## Prompt 4: Chronotype Detection from Passive Device Usage and Adaptive Briefing Timing

**CONTEXT:**
This research supports a macOS executive OS that delivers a cognitive morning briefing to a C-suite executive. The briefing is generated by Claude Opus 4.6 with extended thinking and contains the day's intelligence synthesis — anomalies, predictions, relationship health alerts, and strategic context. Currently the briefing is generated at a fixed time (2 AM nightly processing, delivered at app open). We want to determine whether adapting the briefing delivery and content to the executive's chronotype (circadian rhythm phase) would improve engagement and cognitive uptake. The system has access to passive signals: app usage timestamps, email send times, calendar patterns, and device wake/sleep times — but no explicit sleep tracking hardware. For project context, reference github.com/ammarshah1n/Timed-Brain — especially the morning briefing spec and nightly pipeline architecture in `06 - Context/`.

**CORE QUESTION:**
**Can an executive's chronotype be reliably inferred from passive digital device usage patterns (email timestamps, app usage, calendar scheduling), and does adapting cognitive briefing timing to chronotype measurably improve information uptake and decision quality?**

**SUB-QUESTIONS:**

1. What validated methods exist for inferring circadian rhythm phase from digital device usage patterns without wearables or sleep tracking hardware? Include specific algorithms, required observation windows, and achieved accuracy vs gold-standard measures (DLMO, actigraphy, MEQ).

2. What is the documented relationship between chronotype and cognitive performance windows? Specifically, when during the day do different chronotypes (morning/intermediate/evening) show peak performance for: (a) absorbing complex briefings, (b) making strategic decisions, (c) creative problem-solving?

3. How much does briefing delivery timing affect information retention and actionability? Are there studies comparing fixed vs chronotype-adaptive delivery of cognitive content in professional contexts?

4. What specific digital signals have the highest predictive power for chronotype classification? Rank: first email send time, last email send time, peak app usage window, calendar scheduling preferences, response latency by time of day. Include effect sizes and sample requirements.

5. How stable is chronotype over time? How frequently should the system recalibrate its chronotype estimate? What life events or seasonal changes cause chronotype shifts that the system should detect?

6. What are the privacy implications of inferring sleep patterns and circadian rhythms from work device usage? What ethical frameworks apply? What transparency mechanisms should the system implement?

**OUTPUT FORMAT:**
- A classification algorithm in pseudocode for chronotype inference from digital signals, with specific feature extraction steps
- A table: digital signal × predictive power for chronotype × minimum observation window × sample size required
- A recommended briefing schedule adaptation strategy (what changes: timing, content density, alert threshold, format) for each chronotype
- Decision framework for when to apply chronotype adaptation vs fixed schedule
- Prefer primary sources: peer-reviewed research (arXiv, ACM, IEEE, PubMed), official technical documentation, and authoritative engineering references over secondary summaries. For each major claim, cite the original source.
- Do not speculate. Where evidence is absent, state the gap explicitly.
- The output will be used directly by Claude Opus 4.6 (1M context window) as a technical build brief. Write with that reader in mind — maximum technical precision, no hand-holding, no consumer-level explanation.

## Prompt 5: Adversarial Self-Review Patterns for LLM-Generated Intelligence Briefings

**CONTEXT:**
This research supports a cognitive intelligence system where Claude Opus 4.6 generates a daily executive briefing, then a second Opus pass adversarially reviews the briefing for quality issues before delivery. The briefing contains: day narrative, anomaly flags, prediction updates, relationship health alerts, avoidance detection, and bias indicators. The adversarial pass must catch over-claimed patterns (stating a trend exists from insufficient evidence), missing context (omitting contradictory data), stale predictions (repeating predictions that should have been resolved), hedging failures (expressing false certainty), and engagement self-correction (detecting when the system's recommendations are being ignored). This is not a generic LLM self-review — it is specifically about catching intelligence analysis failures. For project context, reference github.com/ammarshah1n/Timed-Brain — especially the morning briefing spec and the self-improvement pipeline in `06 - Context/`.

**CORE QUESTION:**
**What are the most effective prompt engineering patterns and architectural strategies for having a frontier LLM (Claude Opus 4.6) adversarially review its own intelligence analysis output, and what categories of errors does self-review reliably catch vs systematically miss?**

**SUB-QUESTIONS:**

1. What does the research literature say about LLM self-consistency and self-critique capabilities? Specifically, when a model reviews its own output in a separate pass, what error categories does it reliably detect (factual errors, logical inconsistencies, unsupported claims) and what does it systematically miss (shared training biases, plausible-sounding confabulations)?

2. What specific prompt structures for adversarial review have been evaluated in research? Include: Constitutional AI critique prompts (Anthropic), debate-style prompts, rubric-based review prompts, and red-team prompts. Which structures produce the highest error detection rates for analytical/intelligence content?

3. How should the adversarial pass be architecturally separated from the generation pass to maximise independence? Should it use a different system prompt? Different temperature? Different thinking budget? Should it see only the output, or both the output and the input data?

4. What is the "rubber-stamping" failure mode — where the review pass approves everything — and what strategies mitigate it? Include quantitative data on rubber-stamping rates under different prompt conditions.

5. For intelligence analysis specifically (not general text review), what error taxonomy should the adversarial pass check against? Propose a checklist with definitions, drawn from intelligence analysis literature (CIA Tradecraft Primer, Richards Heuer's structured analytic techniques, UK JIO Analytical Quality standards).

6. How should the adversarial pass surface its findings — inline corrections, a separate quality score, a "confidence-adjusted" version of the briefing, or a brief rider note? What format produces the best downstream quality improvement?

- Architecture decision matrix: separation strategies (same model/different prompt vs different model vs ensemble) with tradeoffs

## Prompt 6: Dual-Scoring Architecture — Real-Time vs Batch Importance Scoring for Behavioural Observations

**CONTEXT:**
This research supports a cognitive intelligence system that ingests 50-300 digital behavioural observations per day (emails, calendar events, app usage) for a C-suite executive. Currently, importance scoring (1-10 scale) happens in a nightly batch: Haiku 4.5 scores all observations, then Sonnet re-scores the uncertain band (0.55-0.75). We are adding real-time Haiku classification at ingestion time — every observation gets scored within seconds of arrival. This creates a dual-scoring architecture where the same observation has a real-time score and a later batch score. The system uses these scores for alert generation, daily summary weighting, anomaly detection, and memory retrieval ranking. For project context, reference github.com/ammarshah1n/Timed-Brain — especially the nightly pipeline architecture and importance scoring spec in `06 - Context/`.

**CORE QUESTION:**
**In a dual-scoring architecture where observations receive both a real-time score (lightweight model at ingestion) and a batch score (heavier model with full-day context), how should score conflicts be resolved, and what are the documented tradeoffs for alert accuracy, pattern detection quality, and user trust?**

**SUB-QUESTIONS:**

1. What published research exists on dual-pass or cascaded classification architectures where a fast model scores at ingestion and a slower model refines later? Include specific systems from information retrieval, content moderation, or clinical decision support that use this pattern.

2. How should the system handle score conflicts? When real-time Haiku scores an email as 0.8 importance and nightly Sonnet scores it as 0.4, which score should the system trust for: (a) alert generation, (b) daily summary weighting, (c) memory retrieval ranking, (d) pattern detection?

3. Does real-time scoring introduce recency bias — overweighting the most recent observation because it was scored without full-day context? What specific debiasing strategies exist for time-ordered classification tasks?

4. What is the optimal threshold for real-time alert generation vs waiting for batch refinement? An email scored 0.9 by Haiku at 2pm — should the system alert immediately, or wait until the 2am Sonnet pass confirms? What latency-accuracy tradeoff curves exist in the literature?

5. How should the system surface score revisions to the user? If an alert was generated in real-time but the batch pass downgrades it, should the alert be retracted, annotated, or silently updated? What does the UX research say about alert revision in decision support systems?

6. What data structures and indexing strategies efficiently support dual-scored observations where both scores must be queryable and the authoritative score changes over time? Include specific database schema recommendations.

- A latency-accuracy tradeoff analysis for the alert threshold decision
