# I am building Timed a macOS application designed to be the most intelligent executive operating system ever built. Timed passively observes C-suite executives through email metadata, calendar structure, keystroke dynamics, on-device voice interaction, and application usage patterns. It never takes any action on the world it never sends emails, modifies calendars, schedules meetings, or executes anything. The human always decides and executes. Timed builds a compounding cognitive model of the executive that gets qualitatively smarter every month of observation. The technical stack is Swift/macOS with a Supabase backend, Microsoft Graph API for email and calendar integration, and Claude API Haiku for classification, Sonnet for synthesis, Opus 4.6 for deep reasoning powering the intelligence layer. There is no cost cap on this system the only metric that matters is intelligence quality. Never suggest cheaper alternatives or cost optimisations.

I need a comprehensive research report on how to design a personal intelligence delivery system for a C-suite executive that ensures insights are engaged with, acted upon, and never ignored. This is not about generating insights it is specifically about the delivery layer how intelligence reaches the executive, in what form, at what moment, and through what interaction modality.

1. Intelligence Briefing Design CIA Presidential Daily Brief, Military Intelligence, High-Stakes Communication Science

I need deep research into the design principles behind the world's most consequential intelligence briefings. Start with the CIA's Presidential Daily Brief PDB its evolution from Truman through to current administrations, the design decisions behind its format, length, lead structure, classification of urgency levels, and the deliberate editorial choices about what gets included versus omitted. How has the PDB adapted to different presidents' cognitive styles and information preferences? What does the intelligence community's internal research say about what makes a brief effective versus what causes it to be skimmed or ignored? Extend this to military intelligence briefing methodology NATO intelligence preparation, operational briefing design, the specific principles governing how time-critical intelligence is structured differently from strategic intelligence. Cover the research on information architecture in high-stakes environments how do you compress complex, ambiguous, multi-dimensional situations into formats that support rapid accurate comprehension without oversimplifying? Then cover the broader communication science research on high-stakes persuasive communication. What does the research say about primacy and recency effects in briefing structures? What is the optimal ratio of novel information to contextualising information? How does the framing of uncertainty probabilistic language, confidence intervals, scenario presentation affect decision-maker engagement and accuracy? What are the documented failure modes information overload, alarm fatigue, the cry-wolf effect, desensitisation to warnings?

2. Morning Brief Design Information Architecture for Executive Intelligence

Based on the briefing research above, I need a detailed analysis of what an optimal daily intelligence briefing for a C-suite executive should look like. This is Timed's primary scheduled output a morning briefing that the executive reads or listens to before their day begins. Cover the optimal structure what comes first, second, third, the ideal length and information density, what categories of insight should be included calendar intelligence, email pattern analysis, decision quality observations, cognitive load assessment, relationship dynamics, emerging patterns, and critically what should be deliberately omitted from the daily brief and delivered through other channels instead. What does the research say about how a briefing should handle items that are important but not urgent, versus items that are urgent but potentially noise? How should a briefing handle items where the system has moderate versus high confidence? What is the research-backed approach to brevity that does not sacrifice comprehension the science of compression in intelligence communication?

3. Real-Time Alert Design Threshold Logic, Timing, and Interruption Science

When should Timed interrupt an executive with a real-time insight outside of scheduled briefings? I need comprehensive research on interruption science, attention management, and alert design. Cover the cognitive cost of interruption the research on task-switching costs, recovery time, and decision quality impact of poorly timed interruptions, the threshold logic for determining when an insight is worth the interruption cost salience, confidence, time-sensitivity, and actionability as dimensions, and timing logic how to detect moments of lower cognitive load where an interruption is less costly transitions between tasks, post-meeting windows, natural break points. What does the research say about alert frequency and its relationship to engagement how quickly does alert fatigue set in, what is the optimal frequency ceiling, and how should a system dynamically adjust alert frequency based on user engagement patterns? Cover the medical alarm fatigue literature ICU alarm research, the cybersecurity alert fatigue research, and any research on executive notification management. What design principles ensure that when Timed does interrupt, the executive takes it seriously?

4. Voice Interaction Design Delivering Uncomfortable Insights Through Conversational AI

Timed has on-device voice interaction capability. I need research on how to design voice-based intelligence delivery, particularly for insights that the executive may not want to hear. Cover the research on vocal tone, pacing, and prosody in persuasive and trust-building communication, how conversational AI should handle the delivery of observations that challenge the executive's self-perception, the sequencing of disclosure what do you establish before delivering a difficult insight, and how voice-based delivery differs from text-based delivery in terms of processing depth, emotional response, and behaviour change. Cover the therapeutic communication literature how therapists and executive coaches use conversational technique to deliver observations that produce genuine reflection rather than defensiveness. What is the research on the disclosure sequence the way information should be structured conversationally to maximise receptivity? How should Timed's voice modality handle executive pushback, dismissal, or rationalisation in real time? What does the research say about conversational repair recovering from a delivery that triggered defensiveness?

5. Adaptive Delivery Learning Which Formats This Executive Engages With

Timed must learn each executive's information consumption preferences and adapt its delivery accordingly. I need research on what feedback signals indicate genuine engagement versus surface acknowledgment time spent, follow-up queries, behaviour change, re-access patterns, forwarding behaviour, how recommendation systems and adaptive interfaces learn user preferences in high-stakes contexts not entertainment medical, military, intelligence, and the cold-start problem for a system that has limited initial data about an executive's preferences. What does the research say about multi-modal delivery optimisation learning whether this executive responds better to visual data, narrative text, voice briefings, or structured bullet points? How should the system handle the difference between what an executive says they prefer and what they actually engage with? What is the research on implicit versus explicit preference learning in adaptive systems?

6. Attention Economy and Cognitive Load Maximum Information Density That Lands

What does the cognitive load research say about the maximum information density an executive can actually process and act on per interaction? I need research on Miller's law and working memory constraints in expert populations, the distinction between germane and extraneous cognitive load in information delivery, how information density should vary based on estimated current cognitive load which Timed can infer from behavioural signals, and the ruthless signal-from-noise filtering principles that ensure every piece of information delivered has earned its place. Cover the research on information triage how intelligence analysts, emergency physicians, and military commanders filter massive information streams to surface only what matters. What design principles govern this filtering in expert decision support systems? How does a system avoid both false negatives missing something important and false positives surfacing noise that erodes trust?

Required Output Format:
Structure the report as a delivery design specification

- A briefing template with annotated structure explaining why each element is positioned where it is, with citations to the underlying research
- An alert decision matrix with dimensions of salience, confidence, time-sensitivity, actionability, and current executive cognitive state, with threshold recommendations backed by research
- Voice interaction design principles a framework for conversational intelligence delivery, with specific guidance on delivering uncomfortable observations
- A feedback learning architecture how the delivery system should learn and adapt to individual executive preferences, with cold-start strategy and convergence timeline estimates
- Information density guidelines research-backed rules for maximum cognitive payload per briefing, per alert, and per voice interaction

Cite all major studies with authors, year, and publication. Prioritise empirical research. Include specific numbers response times, retention rates, alert fatigue thresholds wherever the research provides them.

Here's the Timed Intelligence Delivery Design Specification. It's structured as a complete delivery design document — not a research summary, but a specification with annotated components.

The report covers all six domains you asked for:

**What's inside:**

1. **Intelligence Briefing Science** — The CIA PDB's 75-year design evolution across administrations, with a detailed table of how each president's format differed, what the Trump failure case teaches, and the CIA's own five design principles. BLUF doctrine, NATO intelligence structure, primacy/recency research with specific mechanisms, and the ICD 203 verbal/numeric probability findings (32% vs 66% comprehension depending on format).
2. **Morning Brief Architecture** — A seven-section annotated template (~610 words total) with explicit reasoning for why each element sits where it does — primacy position, recency anchor, middle-section structural protection, and the exact exclusion criteria for each channel. Includes the 2×2 criticality/reliability matrix for routing decisions.
3. **Alert Decision Matrix** — A five-dimension threshold framework (salience, confidence, time-sensitivity, actionability, cognitive state), hard frequency ceiling at 3/day with target 0–1, Gloria Mark's 23-minute-15-second recovery finding, the ICU/cybersecurity fatigue data (5–13% of alarms actionable, 90% of subjects probability-match response rates to expected true-alarm frequency), and detection logic for low-cognitive-load windows.
4. **Voice Interaction Design** — The prosody-persuasion research (aligned prosody drives 66%+ higher human-likeness perception), trust acoustic correlates (R²=0.71 model), the five-stage disclosure architecture for difficult observations derived from Motivational Interviewing, and the three-mode conversational repair protocol for pushback and rationalisation.
5. **Adaptive Delivery Learning** — Full cold-start strategy with phased timeline (calibration → structured delivery → format adaptation → content personalisation → compounding model), 3:1 weighting of implicit over explicit preference, and convergence checkpoints at 90/180/365 days.
6. **Cognitive Load and Density Rules** — Cowan's 3–5 chunk limit, Sweller's three load types, the four-gate triage model, and a complete density table across briefing, alert, and voice modalities.
