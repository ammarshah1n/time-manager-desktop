I'm building the morning intelligence session for a macOS cognitive intelligence layer called Timed. This is the flagship interaction — the moment where the system proves it understands the executive better than any PA, coach, or calendar app ever could.

**Hard constraints:**
- Timed never acts on the world — observation only. The executive decides and executes.
- macOS native: Swift 5.9+, SwiftUI, CoreData, on-device voice.
- Four layers: Signal Ingestion → Memory Store → Reflection Engine → Intelligence Delivery. No cross-layer coupling.
- Intelligence compounds — Month 6 >> Month 1.
- Opus 4.6 at max effort for reflection, no cost cap.
- The user is a C-suite executive who has tried everything. Not impressed by features.

**What the morning session IS:**

It is NOT a task list readout. It is an intelligence briefing from a system that has spent the night thinking about this specific person. The structure:

1. **Pattern headline (10 seconds):** The session opens with a single named pattern the system has detected since the last session. Example: "You're in a Scope Creep Spiral on Project Atlas — three meetings this week expanded scope and none narrowed it." This is what hits first. Not "Good morning, here are your tasks."

2. **Cognitive state assessment (15 seconds):** Based on voice analysis of the executive's initial response + historical comparison. "You sound more energised than yesterday — your voice pace is up 15% and your pitch variability matches your high-performance days." Or: "You sound flat. Last time you sounded like this was the week before the board rejected the Meridian proposal."

3. **Day plan (30 seconds):** Tasks ranked by Thompson sampling ML scores, placed into chronotype-optimal time windows. Not just "do X at 9am" but "I'm putting the Henderson negotiation in your 9:30 analytical peak because your pattern shows you concede more after 2pm" — the system EXPLAINS its reasoning using what it knows about the person.

4. **One question:** "What's the thing you most need to think about today that isn't on the list?" This captures the executive's actual priority, which often diverges from the scheduled one. The answer becomes high-signal episodic memory.
